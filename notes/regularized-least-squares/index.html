Regularized Least Squares

Regularization introduces to an optimization problem prior information or assumptions about properties of an optimal solution. A regularization parameter governs the tradeoff between the fidelity to the unmodified problem and the desired solution properties.

The Pareto frontier

The standard least-squares problem can be interpreted as a method for recovering some underlying object (say, a signal or image) encoded in the vector x0x_0x0​ using noisy measurements of the form

b:=Ax0+w1, b := Ax_0 + w_1, b:=Ax0​+w1​,

where www is random noise and the matrix AAA describes the measurement process. The least-squares problem

tries to remove the noise term www.

But what if xxx also needs to satisfy some other competing objective? Suppose, for example, that we have also available other set of measurements of x0x_0x0​ of the form d=Cx0+w2d = Cx_0+w_2d=Cx0​+w2​, where w2w_2w2​ is also random noise. The "best" choice for xxx is not necessarily (1), and instead we wish to choose xxx to balance the two objective values

f1(x)=∥Ax−b∥2andf2(x)=∥Cx−d∥2. f_1(x) = \|Ax - b\|^2 \quad\text{and}\quad f_2(x) = \|Cx - d\|^2. f1​(x)=∥Ax−b∥2andf2​(x)=∥Cx−d∥2.

Generally, we can make f1(x)f_1(x)f1​(x) or f2(x)f_2(x)f2​(x) small, but not both. The figure below sketches the relationship between the pair {f1(x),f2(x)}\{f_1(x), f_2(x)\}{f1​(x),f2​(x)} for all values of xxx.

The objective pairs on the boundary of two regions is the Pareto frontier. We can compute these Pareto optimal solutions xxx by minimizing the weighted sum objective

ℓ(γ,α)={(f1(x),f2(x)):f1(x)+γf2(x)=α,x∈Rn} \ell(\gamma,\alpha) = \{(f_1(x),f_2(x)):f_1(x) +\gamma f_2(x) = \alpha, x \in \mathbb R^n\} ℓ(γ,α)={(f1​(x),f2​(x)):f1​(x)+γf2​(x)=α,x∈Rn}

Example: Signal denoising

Consider a noisy measurement
